Beyond — Product Design Assignment

Emergency Care Finder

Portugal-Focused Product Design Process

A structured walkthrough of how to design a system helping people quickly identify life-threatening situations and route to appropriate emergency care in Portugal.

Data-driven approach using Portugal's INEM (Instituto Nacional de Emergência Médica) triage system, verified response times, and healthcare structure.

01 / 14

Plan of Attack

4 Phases

Structured approach to validate assumptions, involve cross-functional partners, and deliver a testable solution:

Phase 1 Discovery — Research problem space, validate triage logic, audit INEM data integration feasibility
Phase 2 Define — One user, one critical problem, one sharp problem statement aligned to Portugal's healthcare context
Phase 3 Design — Critical flow: Life-threat assessment → triage classification → facility routing
Phase 4 Test & Iterate — Validate with real users in Portugal, refine based on INEM integration feedback

Why this structure? High-stakes domain (emergency care). Wrong assumptions = system misdirects people. 50% of effort goes to validation, not UI.

Phase 1

Discovery & Research

Building confidence in the problem space through primary and secondary research

Research Methods

Qualitative Research:

  • Contextual inquiry: How do people search for emergency care when stressed? (112 vs. driving directly vs. asking neighbors)
  • User interviews: 6-8 Portugal-based participants (elderly, parents with children, workers in rural areas)
  • Healthcare provider interviews: INEM dispatch center staff, ER triage nurses, urgent care doctors

Research Focus: Validate that life-threat detection is the critical first step, not facility selection.

Engineering

  • Geolocation accuracy per target platform
  • Offline-first fallback for rural / no-signal areas

  • Mobile Web / PWA — Fallback for link-shared crisis access. No install required, works immediately
  • Wearable (Watch) — Apple Watch / WearOS. Trigger P1 gate when phone is out of reach. Haptic feedback + oversized tap targets
  • Responsive Tablet / Desktop — For Operators and Dispatchers in coordination centres

Discovery — User Intent

Where Does the User Meet the Product?

Entry intent shapes onboarding, trust signals, and the first screen. A user who installed months ago as a precaution needs a fundamentally different first experience than someone opening a link mid-crisis.

📲
Intent A — Precautionary Install

Before any emergency. User downloads "just in case" after a doctor, insurer, expat community, or trusted friend recommends it.

Design implication: Onboarding can take time. Set location permissions, optionally pre-fill health context, explain triage logic calmly and clearly.

📱
Intent B — SNS Discovery

Passive awareness. Instagram, X, TikTok health content or a news article surfaces the app. User installs but is not in immediate need.

Design implication: App store screenshots and first-open screen must convey trust + simplicity fast. May benefit from an ambient value mode — non-emergency facility finder.

🆘
Intent C — In-Crisis First Open

Active emergency. User Googles "emergency care near me Portugal" or someone shares a link while a person is injured. No prior install.

Design implication: Zero onboarding. PWA / web must work instantly. No login gate. Location request is the first and only friction point.

🏥
Intent D — Institutional / Referred

Pushed by a trusted source. SNS health app directory, travel insurance, employer, or expat relocation package recommends the app upon arrival in Portugal.

Design implication: Trust is already established. Onboarding focuses on setup — language, location, health context — rather than convincing the user to care.

Core Design Question: Can one product serve all four intents without degrading the crisis experience?

Hypothesis: Yes — if first-open state detects context (in-crisis vs. calm) and routes accordingly. Calm → setup flow. Crisis → immediate P1 gate. No account required for either.

Context

Portugal Emergency Care System

Understanding Portugal's healthcare layers and triage system:

Call Entry Point: 112 (INEM) — 7M+ calls annually; only 20% are true emergencies

Average answer time: 8 seconds

Non-Emergency Alternative: Saúde 24 (SNS 24) for non-life-threatening triage

Healthcare Facilities — 3 Tiers

Primary Care

Centro de Saúde (Health Centers) — Family doctors, routine care

Urgent Care

SUMC (Medical-Surgical) — ER at mid-size hospitals

Emergency (Trauma / Critical)

SUP (Polyvalent) — Major hospitals with advanced intervention

06 / 14

INEM 2026

Portugal's Triage System

New 5-level priority system with defined response times:

P1 — Emergent (Life-Threatening) Imminent risk to life. Response: IMMEDIATE dispatch of Basic + Advanced Life Support Examples: Unconsciousness, choking, severe bleeding, chest pain, breathing difficulty, suspected stroke
P2 — Very Urgent (High Risk) Response: Within 18 minutes — Basic Life Support + coordination with Advanced Support Examples: Severe allergic reaction, serious injury, signs of stroke, poisoning
P3 — Urgent (Clinical Risk) Response: Within 60 minutes — Basic Life Support for clinical worsening risk Examples: Sprains, minor fractures, moderate abdominal pain, fever
P4 — Less Urgent (Low Risk) Response: Within 120 minutes — Basic Life Support for stable conditions
P5 — Non-Urgent Refer to primary care or day hospital

Source: The Portugal News, January 2026 — INEM Priority System Implementation

Data

Emergency Response Times in Portugal

Real-world metrics by priority and location:

P1 (Emergent) Response Activation: 1 minute average dispatch time (INEM 2022-2024)

On-scene arrival varies: 10-20 min urban, 30-60+ rural

P2 (Very Urgent): 18 minutes guaranteed response target (new system)

P3 (Urgent) at Hospital ER: 60 minutes from triage to doctor (SNS target)

Reality: 3-13 hours at major urban hospitals (Lisbon, Algarve during peak)

Source: SNS Portal Real-Time Data, Portugal News 2025-2026

Geographic Variation

  • Lisbon Metro: 8-12 min urban average; 25-45 min suburban
  • Porto: 10-15 min urban
  • Algarve (summer): 15-30 min due to tourism density
  • Rural/Interior: 45+ minutes typical

Design Implication: Users in rural areas need to know if 112 dispatch is feasible or if they should drive to nearest facility.

Phase 3

Critical Flow Design

Core Flow: Life-Threat Detection → Triage → Routing

Key Insight: First question must be "Are you/is someone in immediate life danger?" — everything branches from P1 vs. P2-P5.

Flow Overview

1. Life-Threat Gate (Red Light / Green Light):

  • Simple yes/no: "Is the person unconscious, choking, severely bleeding, or having breathing difficulty?"
  • IF YES → P1 (Emergent) → Direct to 112, show nearest SUP hospital
  • IF NO → Continue to secondary triage

2. Secondary Assessment (P2-P5):

  • Follow INEM priority logic (simplified yes/no questions)
  • Route to: SNS 24 call (P4-P5), nearest SUMC/primary care (P3), or SUMC (P2)

3. Outcome (Map + Action):

  • Show top 3 nearest facilities by priority level and distance
  • Time-to-arrival estimates (walking/driving/ambulance)
  • One-tap calling (112, SNS 24, facility direct line)

Design Principle: "Minimal cognitive load under acute stress." No scrolling, no jargon, one clear action per screen.

10 / 14

Decisions

Design Decisions & Trade-offs

Decision 1: Binary Gate First ("Life Threat?") vs. Graduated Triage

Why: Under extreme stress, people can answer yes/no. Graduated questions create decision paralysis. P1 detection is 90% of the value.

Decision 2: Show Top 3 Facilities (not 10+)

Why: Choice paralysis is real. Distance + facility tier (SUP vs. SUMC) is enough. More = user defaults to calling 112 instead of acting.

Decision 3: Direct Routing, Not Self-Diagnosis Checker

Why: Detailed symptom checkers delay action. Users in acute distress want a clear path, not a medical interview. If unsure → P3 (60 min) or SNS 24 call.

Decision 4: Out of Scope (v1): User accounts, medical history, insurance integration

Why: Speed > personalization. If user has 30 seconds, we don't ask for login. Pre-fill comes after v1 validation.

Each decision prioritizes speed and clarity over feature richness.

Phase 4

Testing & Measurement

Testing Strategy

Moderated Usability Testing: 6-8 Portugal-based participants

  • Scenario: "You've hurt your ankle badly. Find appropriate care."
  • Measure: Task completion, time-to-decision, confidence, clarity of triage questions
  • Location-based testing: Urban (Lisbon) + rural (Interior) for response-time realism

KPIs

Targets are directional — exact values set after Phase 1 establishes current-state baselines.

Correct Triage Rate

85%+

users identify the right triage level

Time-to-Decision

<45 sec

app open → routing decision

Confidence Score

80%+

report "high/very high" confidence

Route Clarity

90%+

understand why they're routed (P1 vs. P3)

Early Warning Signals

⚡ Users hesitate >10 sec at any question → Language too complex or question order wrong

⚡ Users call 112 anyway (even after routing) → Trust issue with app triage vs. human dispatcher

⚡ Rural users report "no facility nearby" → Response time data incomplete or geography mismatch

Next Phase: Post-MVP, gather telemetry on actual user paths + INEM dispatch data correlation.
